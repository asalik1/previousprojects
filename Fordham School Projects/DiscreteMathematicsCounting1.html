Discrete Mathematics: Counting

Introduction

Although counting sounds like something that a child would learn in elementary school, it is something that is necessary to review and practice, even for college students. The basics of counting are focused on addition and multiplication. With enough practice and knowledge of the basics in counting, we will begin working with more complicated topics like combinations and permutations. These 4 topics can be implemented in many different ways in real life as you will see in the provided examples.

Addition Rule

The addition rule is the number of outcomes for each choice added to together. For two choices: |O1+O2| = |O1| + |O2|, where O is the number of outcomes for a specific choice. If there were more choices we would add the number of outcomes for each extra choice we would make. A general idea about addition is that it is used when we see the word ''or.''

Example: In a deck of cards, how many cards are a 9 or a 6?

Solution: There are four cards of 9 and four cards of 6 in a deck of cards. Since we are asked for the total number of 9 or a 6 in the deck, we add the amount of each together. 4 + 4 = 8. There are 8 cards in the deck that are a 9 or 6.

Multiplication Rule

The multiplication rule is based on the number of outcomes per choice multiplied together. For two choices: |O1 x O2| = |O1| x |O2|, where O is the number of outcomes for a specific choice. We would multiply by more outcomes if there were more choices in a question.

A general idea about the multiplication rule is that we relate the word ''and'' to our questions.

Example: If you want to buy an outfit with a shirt, pants and socks, how many different outfits can you choose from 3 shirts, 2 pairs of pants, and 2 pairs of socks?

Solution: Outcomes for shirts is 3, Outcomes for pants is 2 and Outcomes for socks is 2. So you would use the formula, |O1| x |O2| x |O3| = 3 x 2 x 2 = 12 possible outfits.
